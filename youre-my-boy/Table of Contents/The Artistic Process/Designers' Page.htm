Rob Johnson (Lighting Designer)

Comments on designing You’re My Boy:

 

 

 

Working on a new script is always an exciting challenge on many levels.  As the lighting designer on this production, perhaps the most challenging aspect has been to effectively light the sheer number of scenic locations, times of day, and times of season that the script lays out. Add to this the use of a turntable with a neutral-gray translucent projection surface (which configures into 15 different positions around a central pivot point) and the fact that various sections of the stage are often isolated for dramatic effect and the challenges become interesting indeed!

 

The scenic designer has given me essentially a wonderfully blank artistic canvas in the gray revolving walls on which I use color, patterns, and a variety of lighting angles to give us a sense of the 15 different locations.  The trick is to try to use the theatre instrument inventory to maximum effect by building lighting systems that have a degree of flexibility on top of which certain key specials or light patterns can give each scene its own unique identity. Being a world premiere, the script has also gone through a number of revisions and I had to be sure the lighting was designed in a way that could accommodate changes. A number of instruments were also included in the plot that were labeled “TBA” knowing that I would probably find a use for them as new things developed. That has certainly proven to be the case. It certainly has been an experience that has demanded a good bit of creative energy on everyone’s part and hopefully the lighting has contributed in some small way to this interesting and complex endeavor.

 

 

Cynthia Turnbull (Costume Designer)

 

The costume design concept was developed throughout the production meetings in June and July.  We began the production meetings sharing research about the people and the historic period.  Since the characters in this show are real people, in real historical events, there were unique opportunities and challenges for the costumes to assist in defining the characters, the events, and the historic period.   As with all costume design, my goal was not to be an archeologist creating historic period alone, but to create clothes for the characters that provide visual variety and aid in establishing the location and the time of day. Another one of my goals was to emphasize the varied locations and times of day to allow the clothing in this production to vary from robes and nightgowns to formal dinner wear.

 

 

With the color concept for the show I wanted to reflect the nature of the characters, the situation, and character relationships.  In addition, I wanted to reflect the changes in Nixon and Eisenhower and their relationship throughout the show. In the black and white photograph from the 1952 Republican, convention Eisenhower and Nixon are seen with their hands raised above their heads, Nixon in a white suit, Ike in a darker suit. The contrast between the two is dramatic, and this contrast became the inspiration for the color concept between the two characters.  The changes in Nixon and Eisenhower during the course of the show are also indicated through color and color changes in each costume.  There is only one scene II,1 where we see both Nixon and Eisenhower in similar colors. (In that scene they are both in blue). In other scenes they are in opposition visually (brown vs. blue). The color of Nixon’s costumes moves from light summer suits, to a med blue suit as he addresses the nation during the “Checkers” speech, and progressing to Navy as he takes on additional responsibilities after Eisenhower’s heart attack.  Eisenhower’s clothing progresses from navy blue suits, in the first two thirds of the play, to a warmer brown after his heart attack. The two characters switch positions, Nixon becoming the Presidential candidate, and Eisenhower leaving the oval office.  The progression of colors for these characters reinforces that character progression visually.

 

 

 

 

                                        

 

 

 

                                               

 

 

                                    

 

 

 

 

Dan Gray (Set Designer)

 

 

Scenic Design Ideas for You’re My Boy

 

This is a play about politics, ambition, manipulation, betrayal and power. The play unfolds over 8 years (‘52-’60) in a series of seventeen scenes that take us from late-night meetings at various hotels and conventions, to planes and trains on the campaign trail and eventually to the White House. 

 

In initial discussions with Geoff and Herb, it became clear that evoking both the formal/“official” arena of politics and the personal/private/informal spaces where raw emotions and tough choices are confronted would be important to the design.  We also felt that capturing a clear sense of the Fifties in the U.S. would be needed to ground the play in its specific era.  Practically, it must also allow smooth transitions and easy access to the stage for these numerous, disparate locations.  We also discussed raising the Studio One stage floor to allow greater visibility to actors seated down stage (this is a chronic problem with sightlines in this theater).

 

To that end, I started working on a versatile design that could accommodate all these needs.  I began by looking at the essential elements needed for each specific scene.  What was the minimum needed to evoke the location described?  What common threads existed between each of the scenes?  It became clear that whatever the environment was, it needed to be suggestive and not overly specific.  We would also need to choose elements (chairs, tables, desks) that could be used in multiple locations.  From the beginning, it seemed likely that a turntable would be used to help move to these locations.  Projecting “titles” would describe specific locations and help the audience with the chronology of the play.

 

My research helped bring these design ideas more clearly into focus.  Images of the White House and oval office began to suggest a formal composition of elements that could elicit the structure of government.  Campaign posters, buttons and advertising led to a red, white and blue color scheme and a collage of 50’s icons.  The emergence of television as the prevalent media in politics and everyday life set a new frame of reference that found its way into the set.  (Ironically, TV proved a double-edged sword for Nixon.) The lines, distilled shapes and the angular asymmetry of “50’s Modern” design gave me a way to reconsider the formality of the White House that allowed using common, simple elements that could repeat from scene to scene.

 

After several preliminary models that explored all these ideas, we finally settled on the design that appears on stage (and below). In the end, what emerged is a spare, evocative composition that strikes a balance between formal & informal, classic & modern.  Lighting, costumes and sound are instrumental in capturing the distinct looks of each location and completing the stage picture.
