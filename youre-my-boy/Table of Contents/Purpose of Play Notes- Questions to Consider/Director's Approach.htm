Some preliminary director’s notes on

You’re My Boy

 

 

1.                  The play is unusually accurate, historically.

2.                  This is the biographical or historical story of how Richard Nixon’s relationship with Dwight Eisenhower, during the eight years Nixon served as Ike’s Vice President, helped shape him into the suspicious, bitter, confused and vengeful man who gave us Watergate.

3.                  It is also a play about the nature of politics in America, then and now: the impact of money and personality; the impact of the media; the way in which underlings are used and abused, etc.

4.                  The goal is for the characters to be complex: in other words, Nixon isn’t the stereotypical villain we think of him as today, nor is Ike the great white father.

5.                  There is a lot of comedy in the script and more will be developed as the play continues to be rewritten.

6.                  The timespan of the play is 1952 to 1960.

7.                  The running time of the play right now, without intermission, is 2.5 hours.  We want it to run no longer than two hours (not counting intermission), so 20% of the script will need to be deleted. 

8.                  There are seventeen scenes in the play.  It is crucial that the transitions between scenes are accomplished smoothly and rapidly so we don’t lose the audience.

9.                  For budgetary reasons, we will not do the transition voiceovers with slides of the people, but simply as pure voice-overs.  The scene titles (date and place) still need to be projected in some manner, however.

10.              I’d like to see a somewhat abstract set, with “furniture” that is connected to the design of the set and which can function as many different items:  airline seats, easy chairs, train seats, desk chairs, etc.  The furnishings should never leave the stage – simply be rearranged.

11.              I think the abstract set helps justify the dramatic convention that we don’t actually have all the hundreds of additional people around who must actually have been there during the events described.

12.              Getting props on and off rapidly is an issue.

13.              A visual and auditory sense of the 1950’s is important.

14.              Because there is little physical action in the play, I think it is crucial that each scene have a very, very distinctive look and ambience.  If the set is abstract – or at any rate, does not attempt to literally represent each location – the burden is on the lights, costumes and sound to provide variety.

15.              I’d like the costumes to create the sense that the fifties was an elegant or stylish period – not the dull, grey image we sometimes have of that period.  (I thought the film L.A. Confidential did a good job achieving this stylish feel, for instance.)

16.              There should be a lot of costume changes.  Most of the scenes are written so that a character who is onstage at the end of one scene isn’t onstage at the beginning of the next.

17.              There will be a tremendous amount of rewriting between now and the opening night, but the number, order and locations of the individual scenes will probably remain the same.  (Famous last words …)

 

Geoffrey Nelson

June 10, 2005
