Synopsis of the Play

 

Herb Brown’s play You’re My Boy dramatizes the relationship between Eisenhower and Nixon during the period 1952-1961.

 

Act I opens with the behind-the-scenes machinations of political matchmaker Thomas E. Dewey.  The former New York prosecutor and governor is trying to maneuver the nomination for Dwight D. Eisenhower, World War II hero, in the face of support for Robert Taft, who leads the more reactionary wing of the Republican party.  Dewey wants Nixon to become the vice presidential nominee and his discussion with Nixon reveals the Californian’s conflicted loyalties—to himself, the California Republican party, the national Republican party, the nation.  Murray Chotiner, who has advised Nixon on all of his previous campaigns and who has developed the political “dirty tricks” tactic better than any political consultant to that time, counsels Nixon to be careful with Dewey.  And Pat Nixon, his devoted wife, also advises the conflicted politician.  Word spreads that Taft has aligned with Earl Warren, which pressures Nixon further:  He has to decide whether to support Warren, California’s native son, or join the bandwagon for Eisenhower.  With Eisenhower’s chief assistant, Sherman Adams, running interference and trying to block Nixon’s nomination as vice president, and Dewey, Nixon, and Chotiner angling for support throughout the Republican party, Eisenhower—who stays above the fray--wins the nomination.  Eisenhower and Nixon have never met, but Eisenhower is aware of the young politician’s abilities and understands how valuable a campaigner he will be.  Dewey’s maneuvering pays off, Adams loses (he wanted Taft as VP), and Eisenhower chooses Nixon to be his running mate.  Nixon’s wife, Pat, is at first unhappy with the news, but Chotiner convinces her to support her husband’s aspirations.  After the convention, however, a media story suggests that a “slush fund” exists for Senator Nixon, undermining the Republican ticket.  Nixon goes on national television, with Pat on screen supporting her husband, to present the “Checkers” speech.  While saving his place on the ticket, the speech strains the relationship between Eisenhower and Nixon because he 1) suggested that all candidates reveal their personal finances like he did and 2) he did not offer his resignation to Eisenhower, as the general had demanded, but rather asked the American people to tell the Republican National Committee what he should do.  With an overwhelming positive response, Eisenhower has no choice but to keep Nixon on the ticket and proclaim, “You’re my boy!” 

 

Act II reveals the complex relationship between the beloved war hero-president and the political up-and-comer.  Nixon looks up to Eisenhower and performs many political duties the old general disdains (speeches, running off-year elections, battling the ill effects of Joe McCarthy’s red baiting tactics).  The vice president handles Eisenhower’s first heart attack skillfully, but the president does not acknowledge the performance or the many other things Nixon had done for the administration.  Nixon wants more responsibility in foreign affairs—he has the capacity—but Eisenhower uses the vice president mainly for performing the dirty domestic political chores.  Eisenhower’s clumsy involvement in the “dump Nixon” campaign in 1956 campaign furthers Nixon’s frustration.  Nixon loses Chotiner when scandal threatens to undermine the administration.  Then Adams meets a similar fate, but Eisenhower cannot bring himself to fire Adams—he has Nixon do it.  By 1960, Eisenhower is focused on his legacy and, for a variety of reasons that even today most do not really understand, failed to support Nixon strongly in the campaign against John F. Kennedy.  Indeed, the president actually undermined Nixon’s campaign with some ill-considered remarks.  Nixon loses by the slimmest of margins.  While not happy with the campaign’s outcome, Pat Nixon is happy that her husband has promised her—in writing—that there will be no more campaigns.  The play ends with Pat realizing her dreams for a “normal” life will remain dreams and a bitter Nixon vowing never again to be pushed around.

 

The play follows the interaction between two fascinating individuals who not only dominated world events in the last half of the 20th Century, but also attempted, with little success at the time, to reform the Republican Party.  As the relationship between Eisenhower and Nixon unfolds over two presidential administrations, the psychology of both is revealed, including the political acumen of Eisenhower and the flaws in Nixon that, nurtured through perceived and actual slights from Eisenhower and the press, led to his downfall two decades later from the cover-up of Watergate.  The play also reveals, though does not dwell on, the effects of politics on a marriage.

 

Note:  While some dramatic license has been taken in this production (e.g., the location of some of the scenes, people saying lines that others said, compressing time), the playwright has taken care to present the “history” in a way that suggests an authenticity in the characters’ development.
