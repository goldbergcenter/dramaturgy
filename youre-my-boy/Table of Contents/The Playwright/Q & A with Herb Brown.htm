Questions for Playwright Herb Brown

 

 

Why did you write this play? 

 

The psychological relationship between these two, very different types of men who didn’t know one another before running for political office as a team, offers a chance to examine the character of two individuals  who were dominant influences in shaping the world political landscape in the 20th Century.

 

What do you admire in Eisenhower? In Nixon?

 

Eisenhower was called to office by a sense of duty – he’d already achieved more fame and glory than he could get by being elected.  His world view reflects that – he steered a moderate course through the cold war years that kept us from wars that the patriotic extremists (including the Joint Chiefs of Staff) urged him into.

 

Nixon was an extremely competent, master politician.   Scotty Reston of the New York Times described him (along with Lyndon Johnson) as the two ablest politicians of the 20th Century.  He gave Eisenhower the political help he needed in opposing Joe McCarthy and in advocacy for  Civil Rights.  Until Watergate, his handling of the political process is unexcelled, and I believe this to be an important attribute in a President.  Nor was he a right-winger.  It’s forgotten that, in addition to opening relations with China, he advocated a liberal family assistance plan, got OSHA and the EPA started, took us off the gold standard and integrated more schools than Kennedy and Johnson combined.  Can anyone imagine George Bush appointing someone like Daniel Moynihan (as Nixon did) to be in charge of domestic policy?

 

What do you dislike about Eisenhower? Nixon?

 

Eisenhower was image-conscious, shied away from looking bad, and avoided conflict.  As Gen. Walter Bedell Smith put it, he needed a Prat-boy to do his dirty work so he could be the nice guy.  His memoirs are far less honest or revealing than Nixon’s.

 

Nixon was driven by ambition, which often took precedence over policy.  And, his  psychological flaws (paranoia, distrust, isolation) were fed by his experience as a politician to where they became fatal to his effectiveness as a leader – this in spite of the fact that he was one of the most capable men to hold the office.

 

Are you putting politics and ethics “on trial” here, or is that too strong a view?

 

On trial is too strong.  But the interplay between politics and statesmanship; between service and ambition are at the core of the play.

 

Of all of the characters in the play, which one is your favorite? Least favorite?  Why?

 

This I really can’t answer.  It’s like asking which child you like best.  Actually I find something admirable;  yes, even endearing in all of them. 

 

This is a very male-oriented play (only one female is cast).  Was the gender dimension a conscious theme in your writing this play?  Or, it is that way because it was that way then?

 

Not conscious – I would love to have had a better balance in the characters – but, that is how it was in American politics during the fifties.   I do think that Pat Nixon offers a poignant look at a woman who (as she herself said) – sacrificed everything precious in her life to advance the career of her husband.

 

How would you characterize Eisenhower’s and Nixon’s views toward the race issue?

 

Eisenhower was far more naïve.  He thought Brown v. Board a bad decision, made the startling comment at the beginning of his campaign that he didn’t consider race or labor to be relevant issues.  (His personal history, however,  was one of tolerance and fairness toward blacks).  Nixon was sensitive to race questions, became the point man for the civil rights bill, and went out of his way to court black vote.  The question (the play doesn’t answer it and I’m not sure anyone can) is whether this was in service of his ideals or his ambition.  It is true that he formed a new fraternity in college and recruited a black member.

 

How does You’re My Boy relate to the current day? 

This would require an essay, but it is important to me.  A couple of examples from the positions Ike took:  Military  involvement in Viet Nam would get our troops entangled in the jungles and alienate all of Asia.  On British intervention in Suez – We have to go slow here.  The Arabs mistrust us because of our friendliness with Israel and an outbreak of hostilities in the Middle East would be catastrophic.  On the Joint Chief’s recommendation to lay an A-bomb on a Chinese airfield to back them off in the Formosa Strait –  We’d have the whole world and all our allies against us if we did that and when you go it alone in one place you better be prepared to go it alone everywhere from then on.  The application of his world view to present problems seems obvious.

 

In addition (although I don’t want to take this to an extreme because there are differences),  parallels can be seen between exploitation of  the Communist threat in the fifties and  the emotions being exploited in the current terrorist threat.

 

Finally, the values-question is timeless.  Should a President be guided by what people want or by what is best for the people?  The ambition vs. duty-conflict is there even though every action may be claimed to be motivated by the good of the country.

 

The media, or more precisely for the time period, the press, is a recurring theme in this play that operates on several levels.  How has the press/media changed from the 1950s to today? 

 

Though Nixon showed what a television speech could do, TV was not the dominant medium at the time.  We were not into the age of sound bites and conventions staged in TV studios as infomercials.  Until we saw what it did for Kennedy, being a pretty boy wasn’t seen as a major qualification to run for President.  It’s interesting that Nixon, during the years covered by the play, becomes paranoid about the hostility of the press.  Yet he retains trust in television’s ability to connect him directly to the people – no doubt a feeling greatly fed by his success in the ‘Checkers’ speech.   The paranoia is perhaps best seen in  that Nixon’s career shot up as a result of press coverage of the Hiss investigation (and Nixon was at the time credited with good press relations as an asset), yet Nixon grouses that the press turned against him because they never forgave him for exposing Hiss.  

 

How similar and how different is playwriting from your other writing (court cases, mysteries)?

 

Very.  Some of the obvious one are:  A play is far more restricted in the number of characters that an be used to create the world presented; a play is almost all dialog but novels require description and can’t succeed on dialog alone; a play is a much more collaborative process – what the audience sees owes greatly to the director, the scene designer, the sound and light designers, the costumer and so forth.  And, finally, a play comes into being by way of a more evolutionary process.  Novels are re-written and edited, but with a play this process goes far beyond the writing.  It is necessary to know how lines are heard, how the play works when blocked, rehearsed, put in front of a trial audience, etc.  As to legal writing, that is quite different from either.  In a brief, the aim is to persuade and make sure the reader (judge) ‘gets it.’  The best briefs share the values of story, clarity, economy and striking image, but still, the result is something no one would pay to read or see.

 

How different is You’re My Boy from your other plays (Peace With Honor; Mano a Mano, and The Power of God)?

 

Power of God is just a short.  The other plays haven’t been fully developed.  But I do have a keen interest in politics and history, and all of the full length plays deal with some aspect of this.  I am now doing research on Florence Harding, which I hope to be my next project.  I’ve also collected material and some day hope to do something with the trial of the man who shot Garfield.  And if time and energy permit, I’d like to go back to Peace With Honor, which deals with Nixon and the settlement of the Viet Nam War.

 

What is your muse? 

 

I don’t really have a specific muse.  But I guess it would be whatever energizes me when I encounter it.  This  can come from material - for instance my personal psychology makes Nixon the most fascinating of the American Presidents.  And energy can come in a seemingly less related way – the  music of Mozart is one example. 

 

Who are your favorite authors (non-fiction, fiction, plays)? 

 

Shakespeare would be at the top of the list.  I admire Robert Caro as a historian because he is such a good story teller.  There are many novelists – some for the sheer pleasure of entertainment (such as Elmore Leonard), some for evoking a feel for who we are and how I relate to it (such as Tim O’Brien does when he writes about Viet Nam), and some for exquisite observation of character (such as Jane Austen).  This is really an impossible question – one to which there is no answer because there are so many writers I like (and read) for so many different reasons.

 

Do you watch political shows on cable television? 

 

Yes, when there is a significant event.  And I am a political junkie.

 

You have worked closely with Artistic Director Geoffrey Nelson in developing this play.  How would you describe that process?   

 

Geoff has shown me how to present my material.  We’ve worked closely over the past five years and I can’t say enough about how indebted I am to him for his help. He has devoted an enormous amount of time and energy (probably at times quite frustrating to him) into helping me make the transition from novelist to playwright.

 

What have you learned from the workshop process that CATCO implemented to help you develop the play? 

 

A sense of the difference between how the play comes across in my head to how it may come across to an audience.  For example, when something  intended to be taken seriously evokes laughter, or when a line that seemed decent comes out as something the character (and probably no one) would actually say.
