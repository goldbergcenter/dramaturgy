Before the First Rehearsal, the Director presents the “Director’s Approach” to the actors and the other artists involved.  (Notice the point-of-view.)  This presents the parameters within which the company will produce the play. 

 

An Approach to Complete History of America (abridged)

 

By Geoffrey Nelson, Director

 

CHA (to abridge the title) is a fast-paced, physically energetic, broadly comic revue of American pop culture-history from the cynical perspective of the Boomer generation.

 

The conceit is that this three-man troupe has taken on a challenge:  To create a show that is highly entertaining but which also celebrates (and challenges) America’s view of itself over the past several hundred years.  The desire to make people laugh is first and foremost, but the historical content is also important.  It’s infotainment.  (There is also the goal of making history seem fun and not intimidating.)

 

The troupe’s view is that Americans have always had pretensions to noble motives and idealism, but that the reality of our past is not so virtuous. 

 

Most of the “history” in the play is pretty cursory (understandably so) and the (Baby Boomer) authors assume that the audience is familiar with the battle of Concord and Lexington, the writing of the Constitution, the Civil War photographs of Matthew Brady, vaudeville, radio drama, Spiro Agnew, the Vietnam War, etc.  As CHA is almost 15 years old now, some allusions will be lost on people younger than 35.  We will update as needed (as encouraged by the authors).  We want the play to be fun for 12-year-olds as well as seniors.

 

In addition to political/social history, the play also revels in the history of pop entertainment (still photography, vaudeville, radio drama, television, film noir, etc.) which helps define the differences in style between sections of the play.  In order to satirize the civil war photos or radio drama or film noir, we need to spend some time absorbing the style of each form.

 

The humor is broad and a lot of it involves the intentionally “bad joke,” which seems to me particularly American (but probably isn’t).  “Heavy cymbalism” and “the shot heard round the world” are examples.  The pace is fast (we want to catch the audience by surprise – make them laugh before they groan) – faster than The Complete Works of William Shakespeare (abridged).  Think of the old Cary Grant comedy, His Girl Friday.

 

The play also needs to be very energetic – and not just in terms of the actors.  The pace and rhythm of the lighting and sound cues also convey this sense.  Because CHA is very presentational, the acting space is small, it’s raised and it’s right down center.  Because it is interactive, there are steps to the audience.  Because it’s filled with sight gags, it’s designed so that actors can get on and off quickly and be immediately visible to everyone once they enter.

 

The script is structured as a revue: a series of individual “acts,” each satirizing a different event in American history, arranged (like most history books) in chronological order.  The challenge with chronological order is that there is no automatic dramatic-build; the “acts” do not necessarily have greater impact as the show progresses. 

 

In CHA, the dramatic action is actually not tied to the series of historical events, but to the unfolding story of the three characters:  “Tod”, “Jon” and “Ben.”  There are, in effect, two shows.  There is the “Revue of American History” and there is “Tod, Jon and Ben Try to Put On a Revue of American History.”  And it is particularly in the latter show that we need to find our dramatic arc.

 

Although the characters share the names of the actors, the actors are not simply being themselves.  They are playing particular personas – much like the guest stars on the HBO series Extras or The Larry Sanders Show play versions of themselves (usually outrageous and unflattering ones). 

 

“Tod” fancies himself the leader of the group, the intellectual, the accomplished one, the leading man.  He is judgmental, takes himself seriously, and has a high opinion of himself.  “Ben” is the youngest, the most impulsive, enthusiastic and passionate, the one who feels “put upon”, the impatient one.  He is also the least well-read and intellectual of the three.  He and Tod are in natural conflict with one another – both want to “star.”  (In a way, they are the traditional American comedy duo: Rowan and Martin, Martin and Lewis, George Burns and Gracie Allen).

 

“Jon” is the peacemaker (usually).  He is not as openly confrontational as the other two nor as overly sarcastic or cynical.  He is more subtle, more gentle.  He is the oldest and there is something a little retro about him, a little old-fashioned.  He wears a bow-tie, he plays the accordion and the banjo.  Maybe he does impressions (Jimmy Cagney, Walter Cronkite, George Bush).  At first glance, he might almost seem a little nerdy, but as the audience gets to know him he grows cooler.  He is also competing to be the star, but in a different way.  Think Bob Newhart.

 

The actors need to play the characters believably, but the characters in turn may overact and “comment” on the performances they give in the Revue of American History, the “play within a play.”

 

 

Geoffrey Nelson

May 3, 2007
