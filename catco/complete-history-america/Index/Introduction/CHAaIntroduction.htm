Play Notes

It all began in 1981, sputtering on weekends at Renaissance Faires near San Francisco and Los Angeles.  Ten years later the Reduced Shakespeare Company (RSC) had a formula that drew paying customers in England, the former British colonies, Asia, Europe, and the U.S.  The company has produced six plays, two television shows, and numerous radio collaborations.  Essentially, the RSC boys take serious subjects and make comedy from them.  Or so they would have you believe.

London’s West End has been most welcoming to the troupe.  For almost ten years, three productions [The Complete Works of William Shakespeare (abridged), The Complete History of America (abridged), and The Bible: The Complete Word of God (abridged)] ran continuously.

Most of the RSC productions have three actors playing three characters, who may or may not have some elements of the actors in them, and those three characters then play numerous other characters.  Done well, it can be an acting tour de force.

 All the Great Books (abridged), Western Civilization:  The Musical (abridged) and Completely Hollywood (abridged) round out the major theatrical productions.  At any one time, the RSC might have four tours going on around the world.  And theatres produce their own versions.  (CATCO produced … Shakespeare (abridged) in 2005.) 

The current production, however, is different from …Shakespeare.  In the early 1990s, two new playwrights—Reed Martin and Austin Tichnor—joined original RSC member Adam Long, replacing  founding members Daniel Singer and Jess Borgeson, to tell the history of the United States in 90 minutes.  (Martin and Tichnor have contributed to the other productions as well.)  In some ways, the material should be more accessible to audiences:   Americans will recognize some of the material they should have learned in high school; others will appreciate the self-deprecations (or America-bashing).

The playwrights are of the Boomer Generation, and their humor and satire reflect the varied forms of entertainment that has infected all of us in that generation.  In Complete History, they reveal a cynical, dark satire that rests on several themes—American attraction to conspiracy theories; American political correctness run amok; Americans’ impatience with history; Americans’ notorious short attention spans; and American obsession with the personality culture and Hollywood.  There is also a sharp emphasis on various comedy styles.  From an overall context and through at least one scene (Lewis and Clark), they pay homage to Vaudeville; they also include elements of clowning, farce, satire, and black comedy.  The point, however, is to not get too serious about it all (if you must have more information, see Catco’s web site for this production:  http://people.cohums.ohio-state.edu/childs1/HomepageCHAA.htm).  For those of you who hate history, you will have a great time laughing; for those who are History Channel aficionados, you will find yourself laughing at bad jokes that you should not be laughing at.  And by the time you realize that, it will be too late ….
