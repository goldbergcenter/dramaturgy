Play Notes

It all began in 1981—sputtering comedic presentations on weekends at Renaissance Fairs near San Francisco and Los Angeles.  Ten years later comedies of the Reduced Shakespeare Company (RSC) drew paying customers in London, the U.S., Great Britain, Asia, Europe, and the Middle East.  The troupe, originally comprised of actor-creators Adam Long, Daniel Singer and Jess Borgeson, has since produced six plays, two television shows, and numerous radio collaborations.  (See RSC’s homepage:  http://www.reducedshakespeare.com/)

                                     

 

Most RSC plays are written for three actors, each playing a role with some if not all the character traits of one of the playwrights.  Those three characters then take on numerous roles to present serious topics (e.g., all of Shakespeare’s works; the Bible) in about 90 minutes.  Done well, it can be an acting tour de force. 

 

Three productions—The Complete Works of William Shakespeare (abridged), The Complete History of America (abridged), and The Bible:  The Complete Word of God (abridged)—ran continuously in London’s West End for almost ten years!  The Complete Millennium Musical (abridged), All the Great Books (abridged), Western Civilization: The Musical (abridged), and Completely Hollywood (abridged) round out the major theatrical productions.  At any one time, the RSC might be touring four productions around the world.  RSC plays are also produced and performed by different theatre companies. CATCO produced The Complete Works of William Shakespeare (abridged) in 2005.

 

Complete History was created in the early 1990s by Adam Long, Reed Martin, and Austin Tichenor (the latter two had joined RSC in the late 1980s). (See Playwrights for more information.)  CATCO’s Jon Putnam (playing Martin’s role) and Tod Zimmerman (taking on Tichenor’s) return from CATCO’s 2005 Shakespeare production.  Joining Jon and Tod is CATCO first-timer Ben Tebbe, who brings Long’s role to life.

The playwrights are of the Boomer Generation, and their humor reflects the varied entertainment forms that have infected that generation, and those following to a certain extent.    (See Context of Boomer Humor )  In Complete History they reveal a cynical, dark satire that rests on several themes—Americans’ short attention spans and obsessions with the personality culture, Hollywood, political correctness run amok, and conspiracy theories.  There is a sharp emphasis on comedy styles.  The RSC playwrights pay homage to Vaudeville and include elements of clowning, farce, satire, and black comedy.  (See Comedy in the Play)

 

CATCO has added another dimension to the staging of Complete History.  Director Geoff Nelson thought about Laugh In when reading the play and asked scenic designer Stephanie Gerckens to include the “windows” from the Laugh In television set in her design.  Stephanie’s overall approach was to include as many elements on stage related to Vaudeville as possible; thus, she easily incorporated Geoff’s request.  See Designers’ Page for more information on the play’s design elements.

 

The playwrights employ a different medium for most of the scenes:  the “flip chart” for many of the bits; “oral tradition” for the Native American Folklore; “town criers” for the coming of the American Revolution; Vaudeville comedy-dance routine for Lewis and Clark; photographs for the Civil War; radio and movie genres, especially “film noir” for much of Act II. 

 

The conceit—getting through all of American history in 90 minutes—does in part rest upon history.  Some of the material Long, Martin, and Tichenor compiled is historically correct; some is not.  All is grounded in the popular culture of the last half-century and some of that is mixed-up! (Clicking on Historical and Cultural References in the Play will take you to a page-by-page analysis of the script’s references to history.)

 

We should not get too serious about it all.  Many of you will find yourselves laughing at bad jokes that you should not be laughing at.  And by the time you realize that, it will be too late.  These three Americans are moving fast, for they really believe that they can get through the history of their country in 90 minutes.  That is part of the charm—and the comedy.
